Overview

The Problem

Police agencies worldwide lacked a standardized, accessible system for storing and managing digital evidence. Existing tools were proprietary, expensive, or misaligned with the workflows of law enforcement personnel. The result was fragmented evidence handling and accessibility gaps that had real implications for case integrity.

The Digital Evidence Archive is an open-source application that gives global agencies a purpose-built system for digital evidence management. I led the design from the earliest research phase through a successful implementation, working in close collaboration with software developers, TPMs, and specialists in research, design, and accessibility.

Role & Process

How I Worked

This project demanded a rigorous research foundation. Law enforcement workflows are complex, high-stakes, and vary significantly across jurisdictions. I conducted research with personnel across agencies, developing personas and journey maps that captured the range of use cases and user needs.

From research, I moved into wireframes and iterative prototyping, with accessibility as a first-class constraint throughout. I produced detailed accessibility annotations alongside every design artifact, and validated each with specialists to ensure the final product met WCAG 2.1 standards. Collaboration with software developers and TPMs was continuous — this was a product that had to ship correctly.

Key Decisions

Design Choices

Accessibility first

Treating WCAG compliance as a design constraint, not a checklist

Accessibility was built into every artifact from day one rather than validated at the end. This changed the way I structured information hierarchy, color usage, and interactive states throughout the product.
